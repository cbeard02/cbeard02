01 � Overview

The Business Problem

UWM is the nation's largest wholesale mortgage lender, operating exclusively through a network of independent mortgage brokers. Its broker-facing web platform (the command center for thousands of daily lending decisions) had grown through years of product additions without a coherent navigation strategy. Brokers couldn't find tools, couldn't predict where features lived, and were burning time navigating a system that should accelerate their work. Product naming conventions used internal jargon that created a steep learning cliff for new brokers and eroded confidence in experienced ones.

The redesign covered two connected workstreams: restructuring the navigation architecture itself, and aligning product naming conventions with broker mental models rather than internal team structures. Both shared the same north star: time returned to brokers, lent as mortgages.

02 � Context & Constraints

The Real-World Mess

UWM's platform is a live, high-stakes environment used by active mortgage brokers processing real loans. Every design decision carried operational risk.

Entrenched muscle memory

Brokers had years of navigation habits baked into their workflows. Any restructure risked disruption to active loan pipelines if not introduced carefully.

Internal product naming legacy

Product names reflected internal team structures rather than broker mental models. Renaming required cross-functional alignment across product, engineering, legal, and marketing.

IT architecture constraints

Paired directly with IT Architecture and Product Development to ensure UX goals were technically feasible within existing infrastructure.

03 � Methodology

The Framework

The approach combined deep qualitative research with a structured prioritization framework to sequence changes by impact and implementation risk. Three research methods formed the backbone of the navigation strategy: competitive analysis, card sorting, and tree testing.

Competitive Analysis

Before proposing any structural changes, I conducted a competitive analysis focused specifically on navigation patterns across B2B SaaS and financial platforms. The goal was to build an evidence-based perspective on how similar products handled two distinct navigation challenges: contextual navigation (surfacing relevant tools and next steps within a specific workflow) and whole-site navigation (giving users a reliable, predictable map of the entire platform).

Three consistent patterns emerged across competitors:

  • Lender portals focused narrowly on loan submission and management almost universally relied on a side navigation only. The narrower product scope made a single left-rail sufficient for the entire experience.
  • Platforms with a broader service offering � combining loan submission with marketing tools, lead generation, and account management � consistently used a top navigation to expose that wider surface area and let users move freely between distinct product areas.
  • When both navigation patterns appeared together, they served different roles: the top navigation functioned as a universal, always-available anchor to the platform as a whole, while the left navigation was contextual � dynamically reflecting the tools, steps, and content relevant to the workflow the user was actively in.

Studying how comparable platforms solved these problems helped identify established conventions brokers might already carry as expectations, as well as patterns that had failed users in adjacent products. This informed the direction for UWM's navigation strategy before a single card sort was run.

Card Sorting

Card sorting was conducted in three distinct phases, each building on the last. A dedicated UX researcher partnered on this work, using a similarity matrix to analyze card sorting data and surface meaningful patterns in how brokers naturally grouped and categorized platform features.

The similarity matrix quantified how frequently participants grouped items together across sessions, turning qualitative sorting behavior into a structured dataset. This made it possible to identify which groupings were consistent across users (high-confidence IA decisions) and which were ambiguous (areas requiring further testing or deliberate design intervention).

Phase 1: Closed sort baseline

Before any open exploration, we ran a closed card sort against our current core menu items. This established a measurable baseline for how well the existing navigation matched broker mental models, giving us a success rate to improve against rather than starting from assumptions.

Phase 2: Open unmoderated sort

Unmoderated open card sorting sessions were run with a broader participant pool to capture a wider range of mental models without the influence of a facilitator. Participants grouped items freely into categories they named themselves, surfacing unexpected groupings and revealing how brokers genuinely thought about the platform's feature set.

Phase 3: Closed moderated sort

Moderated sessions returned to closed card sorting, this time using category structures informed by the open sort data. Having a facilitator present allowed us to probe reasoning in real time � understanding not just where participants placed items, but why � narrowing ambiguous groupings and validating the emerging IA direction before moving to tree testing.

04 � Handoff & Outcomes

Engineering Collaboration & Results

Handoff was treated as a design activity. Annotated Figma specs included interaction specifications, responsive breakpoint behavior, and edge-case states, reducing back-and-forth during development sprints.

Engineering Collaboration

Paired with IT Architecture and Product Development. Contributed to the Design System, ensuring navigation components were reusable across future product surfaces.

Accessibility

Navigation redesign incorporated WCAG 2.1 AA from the first wireframe. Keyboard navigation, focus management, and ARIA landmark structure were validated pre-release.

Validation

Navigation discoverability improvements are projected at +40%, though live usage data will be needed to define actual gains once the redesign is fully rolled out.
