Overview

Three Phases. One Platform.

What began as a clean-room MVP build of an in-person notarization web app grew, through live usability research and an industry-first pivot, into a remote video notarization platform deployed at the onset of Covid. This case study documents all three phases: the constraints that shaped each one, the decisions made under pressure, and what the product became as a result.

Phase 1 � 2018

Clean-Room MVP: Building the In-Person eClosing App

When Nexsys set out to build its own eClosing platform, we were already using a third-party platform to perform digital closings. That created an unusual and strict constraint: team members working on this project could have no knowledge of that existing platform. No screen references, no feature comparisons, no workflow benchmarking from the current tool.

The intent was to prevent us from simply replicating what already existed, forcing genuinely first-principles thinking. The only permitted reference material was business knowledge: what a real estate closing involves, who participates, what legally needs to happen, and in what order.

No outside platform influence

Team members could not reference, review, or draw on any knowledge of the platform we were currently using for eClosings. A strict information boundary was enforced throughout the project.

Business process as the only anchor

Design decisions had to be grounded in real-world closing procedures (the legal requirements, the roles of each party, the document flow) rather than existing software conventions.

In-person tablet context

The MVP was designed for a shared tablet experience: a single device passed between a notary and a borrower in the same room. This physical context drove fundamental layout and interaction decisions from the start.

Dual-user, single-device flows

The same interface had to serve both the notary (who facilitates and controls) and the borrower (who reviews and signs). Role-switching needed to be clear, deliberate, and tamper-resistant.

The clean-room approach produced a platform built entirely around the actual closing process, not around software conventions inherited from elsewhere. That foundation proved critical when we later had real data to work with.

Phase 2 � Post-Launch

Evaluation & Improvement: Learning from the Live Product

Once the platform was live, we had something we didn't have in Phase 1: real users in real closing scenarios. We ran usability testing sessions with notaries (the power users of the platform, present at every single closing) to understand where the experience was breaking down and why.

Two significant failure patterns emerged from that research. Both were rooted in the same physical reality we had designed around from the start: a tablet, passed between people, in a high-stakes environment.

Failure point: corner interactions

When passing the tablet between the notary and borrower, content and controls positioned in the corners were frequently triggered accidentally, causing errors or, in some cases, unintended cancellation of orders. This was a direct consequence of natural hand positioning during handoff.

Resolution: center the critical actions

Interactive elements were relocated away from corners toward safer central and mid-screen zones. Destructive or consequential actions were given additional confirmation steps to prevent accidental triggers during physical handoff moments.

Failure point: borrower security gaps

If a borrower missed an interaction (didn't tap, didn't sign, didn't confirm), the design didn't always prevent the notary from advancing on their behalf. In a legally binding closing, this was an ethical and legal exposure.

Resolution: enforced borrower gates

Stronger borrower-specific security gates were introduced: states that could only be completed by the borrower, with no notary override path. This preserved the integrity of consent and legally required borrower actions throughout the signing flow.

Phase 2 Outcomes

  • eClosing failure rate reduced from 15% to 3%, a direct result of eliminating accidental triggers during tablet handoff and tightening borrower security gates.
  • High-80s NPS maintained across both notary and borrower user bases following the Phase 2 improvements.
  • Established a feedback loop between live operations and design. Usability testing with notaries became a recurring practice, not a one-time exercise.

Phase 3 � 2019�2020

Remote Online Notarization: Taking eClosing Over Video

The in-person platform gave us a solid foundation. Phase 3 asked a harder question: what if the notary and borrower were never in the same room? Remote Online Notarization (RON), closing a mortgage over a live video call, was an emerging legal concept with new state legislation being enacted rapidly. We were building toward it when Covid arrived and compressed our timeline dramatically.

We weren't just adding a video layer. We were redesigning the entire dual-user flow for a context where physical handoff was impossible, where both parties needed to act on documents simultaneously from separate locations, and where the legal requirements of notarization still had to be provably met.

Accelerated by Covid legislation

Remote notarization laws were being passed at the state level in real time. We had a short deadline driven by the pandemic. Remote closing wasn't a roadmap item anymore; it was an immediate market need.

New interaction paradigm

In-person meant one device, two people. Remote meant two devices, two people, plus a live video connection that had to be maintained throughout the signing. Every interaction had to work across this split context.

Vendor & architecture alignment

We worked directly with product team members and architects to ensure our software vendors could support our UX goals, evaluating what was achievable short-term within sprint velocity, and what required longer-term vendor commitments.

Front-end implementation gaps

Not all developers on the team had sufficient front-end experience to execute the design accurately at pace. This required direct involvement in implementation: paired programming sessions to ensure visual and interaction fidelity.

How We Approached It

Competitive Analysis

RON was genuinely new territory. With no direct competitors to benchmark against, we studied adjacent technology and processes (video conferencing platforms, document signing tools, identity verification flows) to establish user expectations around the type of interaction we were building.

Usability Testing

Testing focused on the main features and process flows to confirm a functional, usable product before launch. Given the timeline, we prioritized breadth (ensuring the critical path worked end-to-end) over depth on edge cases, which were addressed in subsequent iterations.

Developer Collaboration

Worked directly with developers to implement designs accurately under a compressed timeline. Where developers lacked front-end experience, I ran paired programming sessions, writing alongside them to build their capability and maintain design fidelity simultaneously.

Vendor & Architecture Partnership

Teamed up with product members and architects to align software vendors on our UX requirements, distinguishing between what could be delivered in the short term within existing vendor capabilities and what required contractual roadmap commitments for the longer term.

Phase 3 Outcomes

  • Industry-leading RON platform launched ahead of Covid demand: one of the first mortgage eClosing products to support full remote video notarization at scale.
  • New legislation navigated in real time. The platform remained legally compliant across multiple states as RON laws were enacted during development.
  • High-80s NPS sustained through the transition from in-person to remote. User confidence transferred because the core experience was familiar, just distributed.
  • Developer capability raised through paired programming. The team left Phase 3 with stronger front-end skills that benefited all subsequent product work.

Reflection

What This Project Taught Me

The clean-room constraint of Phase 1 felt limiting at the time. In retrospect, it was the most valuable design condition I've worked under. It forced every decision to be justified by the actual process, not by software convention. The platform we built was better for it.

Phase 2 reinforced something that's easy to lose in fast-moving product environments: launch is the beginning of the design process, not the end. The failures we found with notaries (corner interactions, borrower security gaps) were invisible until real people used the product in real conditions. No prototype surfaces them.

Phase 3 was a lesson in designing under genuine uncertainty. New legislation, a global pandemic, an industry that hadn't done this before, and a team that figured it out anyway. The paired programming sessions were unexpectedly one of the most impactful things I did: unblocking developers in real time produced more design fidelity than any handoff specification could have.
